1. Myelin Sheaths The average brain is made up of 40% gray matter and 60% white matter.
White matter lies beneath the gray matter and is composed of long nerve fibers insulated by
myelin sheaths. Myelin is the fatty tissues that make white matter white, and it's one of the
reasons people can get good at things.

    � As you repeat an action, the neurons associated with that action will have their axons
         wrapped in myelin.

    � So every time you put in an hour of practice, you earn yourself another wrap of myelin
         around the neurons used for that activity.

    � More myelin means nerve impulses can travel more quickly and efficiently across the
         axons.

    � This means the action can be done more easily, skillfully, and will require less
         concentration.

    � A bare, un-myelinated neuron will have a signal speed of about 2 miles an hour. The
         signal speed of a full myelinated neuron is about 200 miles an hour.

    � Practice makes perfect because practice makes myelin and myelin makes perfect.

2. Willpower By the 1980s, the theory that willpower is a learnable skill was generally accepted.
It was understood as something that could be taught the same way kids can learn math and say
`thank you'.

    � The amount of willpower you have is finite and it's more like a muscle: you can tire it out
         if you work it too hard.

Sheehan developed his own theory of stuttering, the Approach-Avoidance Conflict Theory.
It is unique in its conception that avoidance of stuttering or the suppression of stuttering is a
major factor contributing to its persistence. He also developed a therapy based on his theory that
stresses openness, admitting one's role as a stutterer, being willing to speak in all kinds of
situations, and learning to stutter in a new and easy way without holding back and without
struggle. Fluency comes as a by-product of changing both attitudes and habits.

Sheehan loved to work with stutterers in groups and often presented such groups for training
purposes both at public meetings and on television. He amazed audiences by showing how
severe stutterers, unable to read a passage alone, could read fluently in unison with other severe
stutterers.

He also helped parents to deal with their young children who were beginning to stutter. His
advice included asking parents to listen to the child, to look at him, to let him stutter, to give him
time to talk, to pay attention to what he was saying, not how he said it, and to never praise
fluency. He urged parents to provide an accepting, understanding environment, free of academic
and social pressures, and one which builds the child's self-concept.

Joe Sheehan was a person of many talents and interests beyond his specialities in speech
pathology and clinical psychology. He was a first rate tennis player, and during his earlier years
as a professor at UCLA he often worked out with the tennis team, sometimes functioning as an
informal player-coach. He was also a talented pianist, and an avid bridge player. Joe's many
talents were also enhanced by a gentle but penetrating sense of humor.

Joe was a warm and kind person who was much loved by all who came to know him well,
students, patients, colleagues, friends, and family members alike. He held strong family ties and
kept in close touch with his three children even though they were widely scattered around the
country following their own careers. As a clinician, he showed the same warm feelings and many
of his patients became lasting personal friends, both respecting him for his professional help and
loving him as a person who cared. In short, Joe Sheehan was a man for all seasons. We shall
truly miss him.
